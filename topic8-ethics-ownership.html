Ethics & Ownership

Intellectual property, software licensing, data protection legislation, privacy, ethical issues in AI, and the wider social impact of computing.

Paper 1 · AS & A Level ~8% of Paper 1 marks

  • Describe different software licensing models and their implications
  • Compare open-source and proprietary software
  • State the key principles of data protection legislation (GDPR)
  • Describe the rights of data subjects under GDPR
  • Discuss privacy issues arising from technology (surveillance, social media)
  • Explain ethical concerns in AI: bias, transparency, accountability

1 Intellectual Property & Law

Intellectual Property (IP) refers to creations of the mind — software, music, written works, inventions, brand names — that are protected by law to give creators economic rights over their work.

Forms of IP Protection

ProtectionWhat it coversHow obtainedDuration
Copyright Original creative works: software code, music, books, images, films Automatic — no registration needed Typically life of author + 70 years
Patent New inventions and technical processes (e.g. algorithms, hardware designs) Must be registered with a patent office; must be novel and non-obvious Up to 20 years from filing date
Trademark Brand names, logos, slogans that identify a company's goods or services Can be registered (™ → ®) or established through use Indefinite, provided it is renewed and used
Trade Secret Confidential business information (e.g. source code, formulas, processes) Maintained through confidentiality agreements (NDAs), no registration Indefinite while kept secret

Copyright in Software

When a developer writes code, copyright arises automatically. This gives the copyright holder the exclusive right to:

  • Copy and distribute the software
  • Modify or create derivative works
  • Sell or licence the software to others

Users must agree to a licence before using software — even "free" software has a licence that governs what is permitted.

Computer Misuse

Legislation such as the Computer Misuse Act 1990 (UK) makes it a criminal offence to:

  • Gain unauthorised access to computer material
  • Access with intent to commit further offences
  • Unauthorised modification of computer material (e.g. installing malware, deleting files)
Exam tip: Know the difference between copyright (automatic, creative works) and patents (registered, inventions). Many students confuse them. Software is protected by copyright, not patent, in most jurisdictions.

2 Software Licensing

A software licence is a legal agreement specifying how software may be used, copied, modified, and distributed. Owning a copy of software does not mean you own the software itself — you licence it.

Proprietary / Commercial

Source code is private. Users pay for a licence to use it. Modification and redistribution are prohibited without permission. Examples: Microsoft Windows, Adobe Photoshop.

Open Source

Source code is freely available. Anyone can view, modify, and redistribute under the terms of the open-source licence. Examples: Linux, Python, Firefox.

Freeware

Free to use at no cost, but source code is not provided and the software cannot be modified. Examples: VLC, 7-Zip (binary-only distribution).

Shareware

Trial version is free; users pay to unlock full features or remove restrictions. Examples: WinRAR, many mobile apps (freemium model).

Public Domain

No copyright restrictions — anyone can use, modify, or sell without restriction. Either copyright has expired or was explicitly waived by the author.

Creative Commons

A family of licences for creative works offering various combinations of permissions: attribution required, no commercial use, share-alike (derivatives must use same licence), no derivatives.

Open Source Licences

LicenceKey Terms
GPL (GNU General Public Licence)Copyleft — any derivative work must also be released under GPL ("viral" licence). Ensures software remains open.
MIT LicenceVery permissive — can use, modify, and distribute in proprietary products, as long as the original copyright notice is included.
Apache 2.0Permissive like MIT but also provides an explicit patent licence grant and requires preservation of notices.
BSD LicencesPermissive family. 2-clause BSD is similar to MIT. 3-clause adds a non-endorsement clause.

Open Source vs Proprietary

FeatureOpen SourceProprietary
Source code accessFreely availableHidden from users
CostUsually free (though support may cost)Licence fee typically required
ModificationPermitted under licence termsNot permitted without permission
SupportCommunity forums, documentationOfficial vendor support
Security auditingAnyone can inspect for vulnerabilitiesOnly the vendor can audit code
ReliabilityVaries; may lack polishTypically well-tested before release
Freeware ≠ Open Source: Freeware is free to use, but the source code is not available and you cannot modify it. Open-source software may or may not have a monetary cost, but the source code is always available.

3 Data Protection Legislation

Personal data is any information that identifies or can identify a living individual — name, email, IP address, biometrics, location. Legislation governs how organisations collect, store, and use it.

GDPR Principles

The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) sets out seven key principles. Personal data must be:

  1. Lawful, fair, and transparent — processed on a legal basis with clear notice to the individual
  2. Purpose limitation — collected for specified, explicit purposes and not processed further in incompatible ways
  3. Data minimisation — only the data actually needed for the stated purpose should be collected
  4. Accuracy — kept up to date; inaccurate data must be corrected or erased without delay
  5. Storage limitation — not kept longer than necessary; retention periods should be defined
  6. Integrity and confidentiality — processed securely using appropriate technical and organisational measures
  7. Accountability — the data controller is responsible for compliance and must be able to demonstrate it

Rights of Data Subjects

RightWhat it means
Right of accessRequest a copy of all personal data held about you (Subject Access Request)
Right to rectificationHave inaccurate or incomplete data corrected
Right to erasure"Right to be forgotten" — request deletion of personal data when it is no longer necessary
Right to data portabilityReceive your data in a structured, machine-readable format and transfer it to another controller
Right to restrict processingPause processing while accuracy or legality is disputed
Right to objectObject to processing for direct marketing or based on legitimate interests
Rights re: automated decisionsNot be subject to solely automated decisions with significant legal or similarly significant effects

Roles Under GDPR

Key Roles

Data Controller: The organisation that determines the purposes and means of processing personal data. Legally responsible for GDPR compliance. Example: a hospital that holds patient records.

Data Processor: Processes data on behalf of the controller — must act only on the controller's instructions. Example: a cloud storage provider used by the hospital.

Data Subject: The living individual whose personal data is being processed. Example: the patient.

Exam tip: You may be asked to identify whether an action complies with GDPR. Key markers: Is there a lawful basis? Is only necessary data collected? Is it stored only as long as needed? Can the subject exercise their rights?

4 Privacy in the Digital Age

Technology has dramatically expanded the ability to collect, store, and analyse personal data, raising serious privacy concerns.

Surveillance Technologies

TechnologyPrivacy Concern
CCTV / facial recognitionMass identification of individuals in public spaces without consent; chilling effect on freedom of assembly
Internet tracking (cookies, fingerprinting)Websites build detailed profiles of browsing behaviour across the web for targeted advertising
Location data (GPS, mobile)Continuous tracking of physical movements; sold to data brokers or accessed by governments
Smart home devicesAlways-on microphones / cameras in homes; data sent to cloud servers; potential for unauthorised access
Social media profilingAggregation of posts, likes, location check-ins to infer personal attributes (political views, mental health, relationships)
Workplace monitoringEmployer surveillance of emails, keystrokes, screenshots; blurs boundary between professional and personal

Anonymisation vs Pseudonymisation

Organisations often claim to protect privacy by transforming data:

  • Anonymisation: Personal identifiers are permanently removed so the individual can no longer be identified. Truly anonymised data falls outside GDPR scope. Difficult to achieve in practice — re-identification attacks are possible.
  • Pseudonymisation: Direct identifiers are replaced with a pseudonym (e.g. an ID number). The link to the real person still exists (held separately). Still personal data under GDPR but treated as lower risk.
Re-identification risk: Even supposedly anonymised datasets can be re-identified by combining them with other publicly available data. For example, combining anonymised medical records with publicly available birth and postcode data can re-identify individuals.

5 Ethical Issues in AI

As AI systems make increasingly consequential decisions, ethical concerns have become central to computer science.

Bias in AI Systems

Algorithmic bias occurs when an AI system produces systematically unfair outcomes for certain groups. This typically originates from:

  • Biased training data: If historical data reflects human prejudices, the model learns those prejudices. Example: a hiring algorithm trained on historical hiring decisions that favoured men will continue to favour men.
  • Non-representative datasets: If training data under-represents certain groups, the model performs poorly for them. Example: facial recognition systems with higher error rates for darker skin tones.
  • Feedback loops: AI decisions influence future data (e.g. predictive policing directs officers to certain areas, generating more arrests there, which are fed back to train the model).

Transparency and Explainability

Many AI models — particularly deep neural networks — are black boxes: their internal decision-making process cannot easily be inspected or explained.

  • Problem: If an algorithm rejects a loan application or a job application, the applicant has a right to know why (under GDPR's rights regarding automated decisions). A black-box model cannot provide that explanation.
  • Explainable AI (XAI): Research area focused on building models whose reasoning can be understood by humans. Trade-off: more explainable models are often less accurate.

Accountability

When an AI system causes harm, who is responsible?

  • The developer who built the algorithm?
  • The organisation that deployed it?
  • The user who relied on its decision?

Current legal frameworks were not designed with AI in mind. The EU AI Act (2024) begins to address liability by classifying AI systems by risk level and imposing obligations on high-risk applications (healthcare, policing, hiring).

Autonomous Weapons & Critical Systems

AI in life-or-death decisions raises profound ethical questions:

  • Autonomous weapons: Systems that can select and engage targets without human intervention. Debate: should machines decide who to kill?
  • Medical diagnosis: AI can match or exceed radiologists at detecting certain cancers — but errors can be fatal. Who is liable if the AI is wrong?
  • Self-driving vehicles: Trolley-problem scenarios — how should an autonomous car make decisions when a collision is unavoidable?
Exam tip: Ethics questions often ask you to "discuss" or "evaluate." You must present both sides — advantages and disadvantages or ethical concerns vs benefits — before reaching a balanced conclusion. Don't just list points; explain the reasoning.

6 Social & Environmental Impact

💼

Employment

  • Automation replacing routine manual and cognitive jobs
  • New tech roles created (AI engineer, data scientist)
  • Gig economy: flexible but insecure work
  • Risk of widening inequality between skilled and unskilled workers
🌍

Digital Divide

  • Unequal access to technology between wealthy and developing nations
  • Rural vs urban internet access gap
  • Those without digital skills excluded from services, jobs, education
  • Risk of entrenching existing social inequalities
♻️

Environmental Impact

  • Data centres consume ~1–2% of global electricity
  • Training large AI models emits significant CO₂
  • E-waste from obsolete hardware contains toxic materials
  • Mining rare earth metals for devices causes environmental damage
🧠

Health & Wellbeing

  • Repetitive strain injuries from prolonged computer use
  • Eye strain, poor posture from screen time
  • Social media linked to anxiety and depression, especially in teenagers
  • Addictive design patterns in apps and games
📰

Misinformation

  • Social media enables rapid spread of false information
  • Deepfakes: AI-generated fake video/audio
  • Filter bubbles: algorithms show content that reinforces existing beliefs
  • Difficult to distinguish authentic from synthetic content
🔓

Freedom & Censorship

  • Governments using the Internet to suppress dissent
  • Internet filtering and content blocking
  • Tech companies as de facto arbiters of speech
  • Tension between free expression and protection from harm

Balancing Benefits and Risks

Exam questions often ask you to evaluate the impact of a technology. Use this framework:

📝 Exam Practice

Practice Question 1 — 3 marks

Distinguish between copyright and patent as forms of intellectual property protection. Give one example of each in the context of computing.

Copyright automatically protects original creative works (e.g. software source code, documentation, graphics) from being copied without permission. It arises at the moment of creation without registration. Duration is typically the author's life plus 70 years. Example: the source code of a video game is automatically protected by copyright.

Patent protects new inventions and must be registered with a patent office. The invention must be novel and non-obvious. Duration is up to 20 years. Example: a novel hardware compression algorithm could be patented.

Practice Question 2 — 4 marks

A company is developing software and must choose between releasing it as open source under the GPL or as proprietary software. Discuss two advantages and two disadvantages of choosing the open-source GPL approach.

Advantages:

  • The community can contribute improvements and bug fixes, reducing development costs and potentially improving quality faster than a single team could.
  • Security researchers worldwide can audit the source code for vulnerabilities — "many eyes" make bugs easier to find (Linus's Law).

Disadvantages:

  • The GPL's copyleft clause means any derivative work must also be released under GPL — the company cannot include the code in proprietary products, limiting commercial opportunities.
  • Without a revenue stream from licence fees, funding ongoing development and providing official technical support is more difficult.
Practice Question 3 — 5 marks

State five of the seven GDPR data protection principles and, for each, give a practical example of how an organisation might comply with it.

Any five from:

  • Lawful, fair, transparent: A website displays a clear privacy notice before collecting email addresses and obtains explicit consent.
  • Purpose limitation: A company collects customer delivery addresses only for order fulfilment, not for selling to marketing firms.
  • Data minimisation: A job application form asks only for relevant qualifications and contact details, not for date of birth or marital status.
  • Accuracy: A bank provides customers with an online portal to update their contact details and regularly prompts them to review their information.
  • Storage limitation: A school deletes student records 7 years after they leave, as defined in its retention schedule.
  • Integrity and confidentiality: Patient records are encrypted at rest and access is restricted to authorised clinical staff via role-based access control.
  • Accountability: An organisation appoints a Data Protection Officer (DPO) and maintains records of all processing activities.
Practice Question 4 — 6 marks

Discuss the ethical issues arising from the use of AI in recruitment decisions. Your answer should consider both the potential benefits and the ethical concerns.

Benefits:

  • Can process large numbers of CVs quickly and consistently, reducing the time and cost of screening.
  • If designed carefully, could reduce human bias (e.g. unconscious bias based on name or address) by focusing on objective criteria.
  • Frees human recruiters to focus on higher-value tasks such as interviews and cultural fit assessment.

Ethical concerns:

  • Algorithmic bias: If trained on historical hiring data that reflects past discrimination (e.g. more male engineers hired), the system will perpetuate or amplify that bias.
  • Lack of transparency: Candidates rejected by a black-box AI system cannot understand why, making it difficult to appeal or improve their application. GDPR grants the right to an explanation for automated decisions.
  • Accountability gap: It is unclear who is responsible when an AI causes a discriminatory hiring decision — the developer, the employer, or the system itself?
  • Reduced diversity: Optimising for similarity to existing employees risks homogenising the workforce and excluding candidates from non-traditional backgrounds.

Conclusion: AI in recruitment can be efficient and consistent, but without careful design, bias audits, and human oversight, it risks embedding and scaling existing inequalities. Regulation and transparency are essential safeguards.

Practice Question 5 — 2 marks

Explain the difference between anonymisation and pseudonymisation of personal data.

Anonymisation permanently removes all identifying information so that individuals can no longer be identified from the data. Truly anonymised data is no longer personal data and falls outside GDPR.

Pseudonymisation replaces direct identifiers (e.g. names) with a code or pseudonym. The individual can still be re-identified using the mapping key held separately. It is still personal data under GDPR but is considered lower risk.

Common Mistakes

  • Saying freeware = open source — freeware has no source code access
  • Confusing copyright (automatic) with patent (registered)
  • Saying GDPR only applies to EU companies — it applies to any organisation processing EU residents' data
  • One-sided answers on "discuss" questions — always give both sides
  • Saying anonymised data is the same as pseudonymised — anonymisation is irreversible
